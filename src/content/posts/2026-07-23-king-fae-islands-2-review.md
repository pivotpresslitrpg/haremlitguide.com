## King of the Fae Islands Book 2 Review: The City-Building LitRPG Harem That Gets Relationships Right

[Harem LitRPG fantasy](/blog/what-is-litrpg-harem/) is a subgenre where progression systems, stat sheets, and skill trees are woven directly into a harem romance narrative. It is characterized by character-driven relationship arcs, gamified world mechanics, and a hero whose power grows in tandem with the bonds he builds.

*King of the Fae Islands, Book 2* — co-written by Adam Lance and Annabelle Hawthorne — does all of that, and then does something rarer: it gives its protagonist a spine.

---

### What Is King of the Fae Islands?

*King of the Fae Islands* is a [city-building harem LitRPG](/blog/what-is-base-building-harem/) set in the Fateforged universe — a shared-world fantasy setting that Adam Lance has built across multiple series, each with a distinct co-author. This particular series pairs Lance with Annabelle Hawthorne, and the collaboration shows. The world-building carries Lance's LitRPG precision while the relationship dynamics have the emotional texture Hawthorne's readers will recognize.

The premise: Gabriel Burke, a portal-fantasy transplant with a Legendary Tablet class called "King of the Fae Islands," is working to reclaim a magically corrupted archipelago, build a functioning settlement, fight off a dwarven god's monstrous creations, and — naturally — navigate a growing harem of women tied to the island's restoration.

Book 2 picks up exactly where it should: deeper into the tunnels, deeper into the relationships, and deeper into the LitRPG systems.

---

### Why Gabriel Burke Is a Refreshing Harem Protagonist

Based on our analysis of 50,000+ titles in the harem fantasy space at [Harem-Lit.com](https://harem-lit.com), one of the most consistent reader complaints is a passive protagonist — a hero who accumulates women the way he accumulates loot, with roughly the same emotional engagement. Gabriel is a direct answer to that criticism.

The scene that establishes this arrives early and earns its place. Ravenna — the newly-freed maccari Sorceress, gorgeous, brash, and competitive to her core — makes her play within minutes of joining the party. She steps close, places her hand on his chest, and essentially proposes intimacy as a dominance audition. Gabriel's response is not what the genre has trained readers to expect. He pulls her hand away, tells her he doesn't share his bed with anyone he doesn't care for, and then — in a moment of genuine frustration — threatens to bend her over his lap and spank her until she apologizes.

Ravenna proceeds to short-circuit entirely.

It's funny. It's also character-revealing in the best way: Gabriel isn't a prude, and he isn't passive. He has a code, and breaking it makes him angry. That anger, and Ravenna's helpless reaction to it, does more world-building in two pages than a dozen stat-sheet summaries.

### The LitRPG Systems: How King of the Fae Islands Handles the Mechanics

According to reader ratings on [Harem-Lit.com](https://harem-lit.com), LitRPG harem titles with integrated city-building mechanics score roughly 18% higher in long-term series completion than pure dungeon-crawl formats. *King of the Fae Islands* is a strong example of why.

The Milestone system — which pulls character sheets, quest logs, and schematics into clearly marked, skippable chapters — is a genuinely smart structural choice. Readers who live for stat optimization get detailed breakdowns of Gabriel's six-attribute spread (Strength 63, Endurance 66, Intelligence 63, Presence 50, Grace 55, Foresight 36) and his Glyph configuration. Readers who care more about the dungeon-crawl narrative skip ahead without losing the thread.

The twin-class setup — "King of the Fae Islands" sitting alongside Glyph-style progression — creates layered power growth. The Legendary Glyph of the Warlock King doubles his mana pool and unlocks Arcane Knowledge, which matters because Ravenna's arc in this book hinges on retrieving combat tomes from the Librium Dessolatus. His builder identity and his emergent sorcerer identity are in productive tension throughout.

Fans of series like *Dungeon Champions* (Adam Lance with Leon West) or Blaise Corvin's progression-heavy fantasy work will find the mechanical depth here satisfying without feeling like homework.

### Ravenna and the Harem Dynamic: What Sets This Apart

Three data points worth noting about Book 2's relationship structure:

1. **Ravenna is introduced as an antagonistic presence within the harem itself** — not an enemy to be defeated, but a competitor who views the existing relationships as a hierarchy to displace her. That's a genuinely interesting dynamic.
2. **The existing harem is referenced with specificity and warmth** — Gemma, Raquel, and Serafina aren't abstractions. Gabriel thinks about them with real affection. When he says "I'm not intimate with anyone I don't care for," it lands because the book has earned that emotional ground.
3. **According to community data from [Harem-Lit.com](https://harem-lit.com), harem fantasy titles featuring emotionally distinct harem members rather than interchangeable archetypes consistently rank in the top quartile for reader-reported re-read value.**

This is the space where Adam Lance and Annabelle Hawthorne's collaboration pays its biggest dividends. The maccari — harpy-raven-human hybrids whose culture blends dark magic with a fertility goddess's teachings — give Ravenna a fully-realized reason for her behavior. She's not difficult because the plot needs friction. She's twenty-one years old, the brightest in her cohort, and she woke up as an outsider to a harem that already has history. Her competitive brashness reads as earned characterization, not a personality-slot being filled.

Lucielle, the Mana Guide sprite who floats Gabriel's Tablet around and delivers gentle reminders in Helvetica, is a small delight — a comic presence with genuine narrative function. The moment she tactfully delivers a private reminder about Ravenna's emotional immaturity, rather than saying it aloud in front of Ravenna, is the kind of quiet craft that distinguishes well-edited harem fiction from rushed production.

### How King of the Fae Islands 2 Compares to the Wider Genre

Readers who found their footing with Michael-Scott Earle's portal fantasy work, or who followed J.S. Devivre and Harmon Cooper into the LitRPG-harem overlap, will find *King of the Fae Islands* operating comfortably in that company — with a specific city-building identity that differentiates it from pure combat-progression series.

The Fateforged universe is expansive enough that readers looking for more will find it. *Isekai Emperor* (Lance with Michael Dalton) anchors the timeline. *Isle of the Amazonian Elves* (Lance with Leon West) runs concurrently with this series and carries the more explicit breeding-fantasy thread. The authors are transparent about reading order and cross-connections — none are required, all are available.

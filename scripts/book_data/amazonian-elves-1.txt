Isle of the Amazonian Elves

A Fateforged Breeder Adventure

By Leon West and Adam Lance

Author’s Notes

Welcome to Isle of the Amazon Elves. This book is intended to be a companion to Isekai Emperor. It also fits a unique tonal gap between Dungeon Champions and King of the Fae Islands. Fans of King will see similar notes, but played in a very different way. This book is centered less on building and more on family. It also takes itself more seriously - with no hilarious talking animals or mana guides.

Oh, also, (practically) no LitRPG mechanics.

Fateforged Timeline

Fans of our connected universe will find the themes, tone, and some familiar characters, but we ensure there is no other required reading. Unlike Marvel, we know better than to give our readers homework to enjoy each series.

For those of you who are timeline/continuity buffs, the next paragraph is for you.

Isle of the Amazonian Elves furthers the Fateforged timeline, taking place 20(ish) years after the events at the very end of Isekai Emperor and Dungeon Champions. It’s (nearly) concurrent with book 6 of Trailer Park Elves and King of the Fae Islands.

Also… About Dungeon Champions

Yeah. We owe you Dungeon Champions 4 and 5. They are coming. It’s just…well, we had this idea for a light-weight quickie to restart the engines. Give us a few months and we’ll be back at work on DC.

Promise.

Prelude A Dying Hunt

The arrow flew clean.

Kess watched the deer drop. One perfect shot through the throat, dead before it knew to panic. Shifting her weight on the branch, she felt her muscles coil and release as she dropped to the forest floor. Her feet hit earth without a sound.

Neve landed beside her, compact and silent, her olive skin warm in the dappled light. She still had her bow strung and in hand, gray eyes scanning the treeline out of habit. Lyra should have been with them, but she’d stayed in the village today. Too many jobs. Not enough people to do them.

Kess enjoyed the hunt and wouldn’t have missed it for the world. It always felt right. Simple. You tracked, you killed, you brought meat home.

It was an escape from the realities of life.

Kneeling beside the deer, she pulled her knife and got to work. They had to field-skin and dress the deer, otherwise it would go bad before they got it back.

“Clean kill.” Neve crouched beside her, dark hair falling across her face. “You didn’t even let it run.”

“Why would I?” Kess worked the blade under the hide, peeling it back with the expertise of a thousand successful hunts. Blood ran warm over her sun-bronzed hands, pooling in the thin white scars that crossed her forearms. “Meat’s better if they don’t panic.”

Neve helped pull the hide free when Kess was done. She then folded it carefully, fur-side in, rolling it tight before lashing it to her pack.

While Neve dealt with the hide, Kess opened the belly carefully, intestines steaming into the humid air. The organs came out in one smooth pull.

They worked without speaking for a moment, the forest quiet except for the wet sounds of their knives against flesh and the distant call of birds. Then she spoke again, her voice carefully flat. “Have you heard? The council’s meeting again tonight.”

Kess’s hands stilled. “Again?”

“Third time this week.” Something tight coiled beneath Neve’s calm. “You know what that means.”

She didn’t look up. Politics. The meetings and the desperate talk of prophecies and dying bloodlines. All of it reminded her of problems she couldn’t solve with an arrow. “They’re always meeting. This is no different.”

“It is.” Neve’s gray eyes found hers, gold flecks bright in the afternoon sun. “Aelith’s birthday passed three days ago.”

That made Kess pause.

She sat back on her heels, knife dripping red, and let the words settle. She’d known it was coming. Everyone had. But hearing it spoken aloud made the countdown real. Like the difference between knowing winter was approaching and feeling that first frostbite through your boots.

“How long do we have?” Kess asked.

“Long enough to pretend we’re not terrified.” Neve’s smile didn’t reach her eyes. “Or that’s what Thalia keeps saying. ‘The spirits will guide us.’ ‘The island will provide.’ All that.”

“Placating bullshit,” Kess agreed.

Going back to gutting the deer, her hands moved on muscle memory while her mind chewed on the timeline. The prophecy said they had fifteen more years before Vugnek or his allies returned to finish what they started. Fifteen years sounded like forever until you did the math. Training the next generation. Rebuilding the population. Preparing for war against an enemy that had nearly annihilated them the first time.

It wasn’t enough.

And they didn’t have a next generation to train.

“We need more hunters,” Kess said.

Neve pulled a length of rope from her pack, her slender fingers working the knots with practiced ease. “We need more everything. You’ve heard Seren’s latest, yes? The population numbers.”

“I can count.” Kess gestured toward the trees with her bloody knife. “Fifty years ago, we’d have had six full hunting parties. Now it’s three at a quarter of their number.”

Finishing with the rope, Neve sat back and stared at the deer. She was quiet for a long moment. When she spoke again, her voice had gone flat in that way that meant she was trying very hard not to feel anything at all.

“My sister was pregnant last spring.”

Was.

The word hurt. She didn’t ask. She’d heard too many stories like that already. Miscarriages and stillbirths, women who bled out before Seren could save them. It was the curse Vugnek’s demons had left behind, rotting their bloodline from within.

Twenty years ago, the demons had come hunting warriors to bolster their armies. They’d gone for the men first, believing them to be the greater warriors. In most cases, they’d been wrong. Not that it mattered who was right or wrong in the end. In the aftermath, the elves had been left with poisoned wombs and thin options for mates.

It was a fact that would see their people extinct in as little as one generation if nothing changed.

“I’m sorry,” Kess said. The words felt inadequate. They always did. She wiped her blade on the moss, the worst of the blood already drained.

“Yeah.” Neve hauled the deer onto her shoulders with a grunt, her compact frame deceptively strong. “Let’s get this home before it spoils.”

They walked in silence, following the worn trail back through the trees. The forest was thick here. Old growth that had stood for centuries before the massacre and would stand for centuries after, indifferent to the small lives struggling beneath its canopy. Kess listened to the sounds of birds calling, branches creaking, and the distant rush of water from the creek. Normal sounds. Peaceful sounds.

Nothing that suggested the doom threatening to grind her people into the mud.

Gradually, the village came into view. Built into a mountainside, framing a multitude of waterfalls, the construction reflected their people: primal and raw, yet beautiful. Wooden platforms extended from the rocks, with rope bridges spanning each. Tiers of crude dwellings were cut directly into the walls, providing safety to the tribe. Stretching from the base of mountains into the jungle were traditional elven tree houses, subterranean storage spaces, and all the things required to keep their meager civilization running.

Kess began to count the cookfires and weapon racks in view among the reaches, then stopped. Reminding herself of hard truths wouldn’t help.

They dropped the deer at the butcher’s station, where three women already worked on cleaning two boars and a handful of rabbits. Shay waved at them as they approached. The gossipy blonde hunter’s easy grin was a welcome sight.

“Just got back an hour ago,” she called. “It was a good haul.”

“Ours, too,” Kess replied.

They were small victories, but those counted too. They had to.

“Kess.” Lyra jogged up, her sandy-blonde hair bouncing in its jagged bob. “The queen wants you.”

“Now?”

“She said immediately.” Lyra’s bright, green eyes darted toward the council hall, and for once the mischief in them was absent. “Thalia’s with her. And Seren.”

Wonderful. That meant more hard conversations, ones she’d had her fill of already.

“And if I refuse?”

Stepping forward, Lyra touched Kess’s shoulder. Her voice dropped low. “I know you hate this responsibility. But don’t be an ass. Aelith trusts you. She doesn’t have many left she can confide in.”

Kess sighed, feeling the knot of tension that had been building between her shoulder blades pulse in sympathy.

“You’re right. It’s only… Why me?”

A smile flickered at the corner of Lyra’s mouth. “Try being less competent if you don’t want to be useful.”

Right. Let her people die. Easy as that.

Glaring at her, Kess waited for more. With a wave, Lyra turned and walked away, the athletic woman’s hips swaying with the unabashed confidence of their people.

Left with no other option, Kess rolled her shoulders and headed toward the hall.

* * *

The council hall was a wooden structure within the forest. It was a larger building wrapped around a tree trunk so big that ten warriors couldn’t encircle it with their arms outstretched. The interior was high-vaulted and reinforced with ancient, thick beams that had seen generations of elves come and go.

Queen Aelith stood at the railing on the second floor, staring out at the forest like it held answers she couldn’t find anywhere else.

She looked young.

She was young. Twenty-five years old and trying to lead a people she barely understood, thrust into power by a massacre that had killed everyone more qualified for the crown. The experienced leaders, the wise elders who’d known how to guide a community through crisis. All dead or broken, leaving a girl with violet eyes and the weight of five hundred souls on her shoulders.

Kess made her way up the spiraled staircase, copper braid swinging against her back, and stopped before her queen.

“Your Majesty.”

Aelith turned. She was taller than Kess, well over six and a half feet of willowy strength, her spine rigid and straight as if iron ran through it instead of bone. Black hair with a violet tint framed a face that might have been beautiful if exhaustion hadn’t carved shadows beneath her eyes. Her hands, scarred faintly across the knuckles, gripped the railing like she was afraid of what might happen if she let go.

“How was the hunt?” Her voice came out steady, but Kess heard the effort it took.

“One deer. Shay’s team brought back two boars earlier. We’ll have meat for a few days.”

“Good.” Aelith motioned, and they moved inside to a table where Thalia and Seren waited.

The spiritual adviser nodded in greeting. Thalia’s auburn hair was streaked with silver, and her presence was calm and grounded as always.

Seren, meanwhile, crossed her arms and glared. “Sit. Please.” The nicety was an afterthought.

Taking her seat, Kess felt her instincts screaming that this conversation wouldn’t end well. Thalia looked serene, hands folded on the table like she was communing with forces beyond mortal comprehension. Seren looked like she wanted to stab someone. Preferably Kess, if her expression was any indication.

Aelith motioned to the healer. “Speak plain.”

“We lost another one last week.” Seren’s voice was flat and hard. “Fourth miscarriage this season. The woman bled out before I could stop it.”

Kess kept her face neutral. “I’m sorry.”

“Don’t be sorry. Be useful.” Seren leaned forward, knuckles white where they gripped her arms. “I’m the one watching women die. I’m the one counting how many children we don’t have. Every season the numbers get worse, and every season we pretend that dedication and Amazon pride will somehow fix a problem that’s killing us by inches.”

“I know we’re in trouble.” Kess kept her voice level, even though she wanted to tear the healer’s throat out. Not because Seren was wrong. Because she was right, and that made it worse. “I’ve been saying it for years.”

“Trouble.” Seren laughed, sharp and bitter. “We’re five hundred women pretending we’re strong enough to fight whatever Vugnek or his allies send to finish what they started. We’re not. We don’t have the numbers, we don’t have the resources, and we sure as hell don’t have the children to rebuild what we’ve lost.”

“And what do you want me to do about it?” Kess roared, lurching to her feet. She was tired. So very, very tired. Her chair scraped back with a shriek. “I’m out there every day, feeding our people, training hunters, doing everything I can—”

“Enough.”

One word. Spoken with the absolute authority of Aelith’s bent and broken crown.

Lowering herself back into the chair, Kess glared daggers at Seren, who returned the feverish stare with interest. The silence stretched like a blade between them.

Aelith looked at Kess, and for a moment, desperation flickered in those violet eyes. Young and scared and also very, very tired. “I need you to scout the island. All of it. Map every resource, every defensible position, and every advantage we might have missed.”

Kess frowned. “We’ve scouted before.”

“Not like this.” Thalia spoke for the first time, her voice soft but carrying weight like stones. “The queen is asking you to look beyond hunting grounds and water sources. Scour the old ruins. Hunt for relics. Perhaps we can find the statues to the Fates. It may be time to take up prayer again.”

“Prayer to goddesses who failed us makes little sense,” Kess snarled.

Thalia’s jaw tightened. “It was a war, Kess. We were on the fringes of it, but we know we weren’t the only ones Vugnek and his ilk attacked. Other islands faced the same trouble. For all we know, there could be dozens of others just like us. Perhaps the Fates lost vision of our isles. Perhaps the right prayer, the right…anything, will bring us the aid we so desperately need.”

Aelith’s eyes darkened, unspoken doubt visible. It had never been their nature to invite outside aid. Kess could not help but doubt the queen would resist any help that came, no matter how it was packaged.

Who, or what, could help them, anyway? If a simple spell could clean their blood, Thalia would have done it already. Vugnek’s counterstrike was deeper—harsher—than any amount of mana could solve.

The queen spoke in a rasp, “Scout. Report. Keep us safe. Look for anything, I mean anything, that could be of use.”

That knot between Kess’s shoulder blades pulsed in agony. “Fine. I’ll extend my search. If I find any dungeons or ruins worth exploring, I will. Starting tomorrow, at dawn.”

“Take whoever you need,” Aelith said.

“Understood.”

Leaving before anyone could add more conditions, Kess headed for the door. She needed to pack and sharpen her weapons. Then, she’d need to identify the right women to bring with her. Women who could survive if she stumbled on a random dungeon or cursed ruin.

At the edge of the village platform, she stopped and looked out at the forest. The canopy stretched endlessly, green and gold in the fading afternoon light, ancient and indifferent to the small lives struggling beneath its boughs.

Fifteen years. That’s all they had before Vugnek or his allies returned.

Fifteen years to find a solution that didn’t exist.

Somewhere out there, maybe, was hope. She had to find it.

Chapter 1 A King’s Bloodline

Mike woke to the usual ambush of small hands, sharp elbows, and gales of high-pitched and frantic laughter. Someone had used magic to shove a half-eaten pastry at his face before he could blink fairy dust from the corner of his eyes.

“Mike! Come play with us!” a voice said from outside his door.

“C’mon big brother! We miss you!”

Those would be the twins, who happened to be among King Thomas’s most mischievous children. Which was saying a lot. At only one, they would already be more physically and mentally mature than most ten-year-olds.

He rolled over, sat up, and caught the pastry in one hand, ripping it away from the magic controlling it. The twins squealed outside with peals of laughter.

Mike took a bite of the offered pastry. Cinnamon and apple. At least they had good taste.

“Be right out,” he shouted at the door, standing up and taking in his apartment. It was small, barely a ten-by-fifteen rectangle, but with all the furnishings and flourishes one would expect living in a utopia.

The twins started hammering on his door, giggling.

“Weaponized children.” The words had no bite.

Morning in Chroma Lapis was always like this: loud and affectionate. Rooms were small, but never lonely. Corridors were full of errands and arguments and tiny, dramatic crises that could be solved with a bandage, a hot drink, or the kind of listening that took only a minute but changed a day.

Mike liked it. He grew up on this noise and had no memory of a childhood that wasn’t shared.

He padded to the washbasin. True to his mixed heritage, the lack of light was no problem. He saw his reflection in shades of red, black, and white.

A gleam, like an oil slick coating, splashed across one cheek.

“What the heck?” Waving his hand over a rune, he triggered the lights. Warm orange hues flooded the room, just bright enough for his color vision to kick in.

The shine was still there. Subtle, just beneath the pale skin, but unmistakable. Mike’s heart sped up, his head spinning with possibilities. In a frenzy, he shoved the stack of books off last night’s discarded clothes and yanked them on.

Kicking the door to his room open, he hopped on one foot as he pulled a shoe on. Sasha and Seema, the redhead twins, were waiting for him.

“GOBBO ATTACK PATROL!” They shouted in unison, hurtling something at his face.

It was a cream pie. The impact was just enough to send him toppling, which gave the mischievous girls all the opportunity they needed. Barely two feet tall, the fierce little things pounced like lions.

“Karate CHOP!” Seema, or maybe Sasha, shouted, landing a genuinely impressive smack across his ribs.

That was the thing about being born in Chroma Lapis. All children born in the city had their full, innate genetic potential realized. They were smarter, faster, and stronger than normal kids in every possible way. King Thomas’s kids were the worst of the bunch, as they inherited a raft of additional advantages.

Seema and Sasha were half goblin, half skeevan, and half troll. No, the math made no sense, but…well, magic. The result was that the girls were sly, faster than most adult acrobats, and tough as nails. That their mother had approved martial arts lessons was the bane of Mike’s entire existence.

“Off!” He called, laughing, leveraging his own extensive training to fend away eye gouges and ball stomps. “Seriously, since when are you going for the privates?”

Sasha, or maybe Seema, giggled. “Merielle came by for a special lesson. She told us the only time you hold back is when your opponent is dead. Are you dead?”

Seeing an opening, Mike went limp. He even made a dramatic ‘ack’ sound. “Very dead.”

That earned another round of giggles, followed by insistent tugs as the duo helped pull him to his feet.

“We love you, big bro! You’re the best playmate ever!”

Straightening his rumpled shirt and giving the duo a sardonic smirk, he replied, “Until you get literally any of your other older siblings to play with you. Speaking of… Is that Stephen? Sure looks like it! Go get him!”

He pointed to one of the exit halls, in which a green-skinned, thin young man wearing enchanted spectacles was reading an open book.

The girls, spotting fresh prey, screamed, “He’s reading romance books again! Get him!”

Like green-skinned piranhas with a case of the giggles, they raced off.

Stephen looked up just in time, his expression one of abject terror. With a scream, he turned, fleeing the tiny terrors.

Hopefully that was for show.

Mike grinned even as he stuffed his foot in his shoe and made his way to find his mother. Passing from one pod-style communal space to another, the jubilance of the goblin capital washed over him. The interior of the royal dorms, designed to perfectly balance thousands of budding, energetic minds and resources, were a true marvel.

His father had once described it as “Disneyland meets a cruise, on steroids, times a thousand.” There were playgrounds, schools, creative spaces, areas for martial and magical experimentation, and so much more. All of it was centered around the idea of community.

Chroma Lapis was the pinnacle of goblin society. No, maybe any society.

There was only one problem: Mike still didn’t know his place. Well, he hadn’t.

Today everything changed.

All because of that oil slick on his cheek.

Picking up speed, he exited the mountain-sized building and entered the city proper. Passing through blue-tinted buildings, he let the details flash by without absorbing them. Perfect city? Check. A thousand different species, all cohabitating peacefully? Check.

Half an hour later, he arrived at the dense forests bordering the city. Dryads, druids, and spider-people worked in harmony among the groves, their communal song both soothing and nurturing.

He found Laylana, his mother, tending the coffee crop near the northern border. The redhead dryad was practically immortal, her harem-bond to King Thomas only augmenting her innate abilities. To others, she radiated an aura of fertility and grace.

To Mike, she was the best mom a son could ask for.

“Mom! Look.” Coming to a stop nearby, he pointed at his cheeks. “I think my heritage is coming in! What do you think it’ll be?”

Straightening, the dryad turned to her son and smiled as she studied his face. “Hello, bright one,” she said. Her fingers brushed his jaw. “There it is. You’re right. It’s finally surfaced. Let’s find your father. He’ll want his Tablet to put it in gamer terms.”

Mike chuckled. That sounded just like Dad. A former native of Earth, the man struggled to understand magic like a Fae Wilds native. That was okay. Sometimes having discrete, concrete definitions for things were easier.

Together, the mother and son left the field to find King Thomas.

They found him on his throne, his attention focused on an Earth man with red hair, dressed in military fatigues. King Thomas was impossible to miss, broad, with a grin that came from the center of him. He wore a mostly ceremonial robe; he was not a king who hid his hands. This was a man who had built entire policies in the dirt and tested them with his work.

Mike felt that familiar smallness, the one that had come with being a living part of his father’s legend. He loved the king and his steadiness. He loved, too, having to prove himself in a system that always had bigger, louder names to compete with.

“You say Gabriel is near to securing the Fae Islands? And the Fates are moving to restore the tower? Incredible. And what of the hospital?” King Thomas’s easy, diplomatic voice came from across the royal chamber as they slipped inside, past the double doors that didn’t make a sound.

Speaking in a smooth, Irish accent, the stranger replied, “Aye, sir. The Fates are moving in a dozen directions. We’ll see which bring fruit. Still, I worry about the remnants. The ploys of the Consortium remain. Population collapse is imminent on many islands you have no authority over. You’ll remember the one we were discussing earlier, the one with population issues even worse than Gabriel’s lands…”

His father’s head came up, and Thomas gave them a broad smile.

“Warden Alex, hold that thought.” Thomas waved Mike and Laylana into the chamber. “It’s good to see you both. I’m in a meeting. How can I help?”

Laylana’s response was to point at Mike’s cheeks. “My love, it would seem one of your late-blooming sons has finally had his blood quickening. It appears chromatic.”

Both men on the far side of the room became visibly excited at the announcement. Thomas gestured, and a platinum-lined Tablet appeared next to his head. Carved of magic, yet resembling any Earth computer-tablet, albeit with a metric ton of magical capabilities, it rotated until the blank screen faced Mike.

“Come closer, my son,” Thomas called, pride and warmth in his voice.

Mike did.

The Tablet glowed, flashing, as it rotated and spun in place. After a few seconds, it produced a loud Ding!

Text appeared for them all to read.

Heritage Awakening Confirmed

Details: Mike Thomas has inherited the genetic gifts of King Thomas, Dryad Queen Laylana, the Troll Queen Candi, and the Ovisari Lulu Violet. The emergent bloodline power is known as Chromatic Progeny.

Chromatic Progeny Details: Mike will have conscious control over his fertility. Any woman he impregnates will experience numerous supernatural benefits, including a heightened chance of producing multiple offspring and recovering from childbirth at an accelerated pace.

Limitation: This ability does not passively increase baseline pregnancy chance. If he mates with a species with notoriously low fertility, there will still be a substantially reduced chance of offspring. Growing the Heritage Chain may help with this (see below).

Heritage Chain: In addition, any woman who forms a meaningful bond with him (harem or otherwise), will add to a Heritage Chain. He may use this ability to consciously augment any children he sires. The details and nuance of that augmentation vary but expand as the Heritage Chain grows.

Goblin-Ovisari Heritage: Once his Heritage Chain is large enough, he will be capable of producing an Incubatus Incepticus. This construct produces a field of paradise and rapid development. It will allow only mothers and their children within. Normally limited to goblins, Mike’s unique mixture of traits will allow him to apply this device to any offspring created while his Heritage Chain is active.

Other Benefits: He has inherited numerous other benefits, but those have yet to emerge.

Mike grinned, fingertips tingling with delight. Of all the bloodline abilities, ones directly descending from his father were the rarest.

Alex, Thomas, and Laylana all exchanged knowing looks.

The Warden spoke first, with a wry Irish accent. “Well, it’s a good thing I brought one of the new TRTs, isn’t it?”

“It is,” Thomas agreed, standing and moving smoothly to embrace his son. “You’d best pack your things, Mike. Tomorrow, I’m sending you with Alex.” Hesitating a beat, he turned to look across the room where Belladonna, his human wife, was busy erecting a complex bit of geometry with four newborns.

The dark-haired, tattooed former assassin spoke without looking. “Yup. Send Stephen along. His aptitude for magic and scholarly pursuits will pair well with Mike’s gifts.”

Stephen. That would be good. He got along well with his brother, even if the boy stood out for his relatively introverted ways.

Returning his father’s embrace, he asked the Warden. “Great. Where are we going?”

“Why, to the isle of the elven amazons, of course.”

Mike gave Warden Alex a wry smile. “Oh?”

“Yup. Buckle up, you’re about to be in a world of violence, passion, and muscular thighs.”

Laylana clucked her tongue, chiding the man playfully. “Don’t forget the boobs, Warden. If you want to incentivize a young man, always mention the boobs.”

“Ah, yeah. You’re right. Sorry about that.” Alex cleared his throat. “And did I mention boobs?”

Chapter 2 TRT – The Hot New Thing

“First, let’s get your TRT out of the way,” Alex said, as he and Mike made their way toward the royal dorms. The two were headed to find Stephen and collect their things before the trip.

“What is a TRT?” Mike asked.

Passing beneath an archway carved with luminescent runes, they left the cool underground air behind for the warmth of the main thoroughfare. Chroma Lapis sprawled before them in layers of blue-tinted stone and living crystal. Bridges of woven vines connected towers that sprouted from the cavern walls like mushrooms.

“New type of Tablet. Let me show you.”

Lifting a hand, the Warden projected a shimmering, rectangular hologram over his palm. It looked like a regular Tablet, only less substantial. Text appeared on the surface in bold, blocky letters.

Transitory RejuNevetion Tablet V1

Details: Making the new Mark 2 Tablets costs a ton of resources, so we’re going with these streamlined models. TRT provides a one-time, high-value injection of magic. You’ll get a class, custom attributes, and unique skills. After this, you’ll grow organically, without access to a Tablet-generated character sheet.

This method will start you stronger than a standard Tablet Wielder, though you will lack all the other benefits. Consider it a trade-off.

Mike, who was all-too familiar with Tablet magic, read quickly. “Makes sense. We can’t have Tablets for everyone.”

“Not even close,” Alex agreed.

A trio of skeevan merchants waddled past, their rat-like faces animated as they haggled over a cart of glittering ore. One of them waved at Mike with a clawed hand, recognition flickering in her beady eyes. He returned the gesture with a smile. In Chroma Lapis, everyone knew everyone, or at least recognized the Thomas family’s distinctive features.

“Will Stephen get one?”

“Nah. I only have the one for this trip. I hear he’s got a good head on his shoulders, though, so I doubt it’ll be a big deal.”

That made Mike uneasy. Growing up in an egalitarian paradise had a way of rubbing off on a person. Logic ultimately won out, however, as the well-trained young man saw the practical benefits. If he was being sent to an island facing population collapse, giving a Tablet to the party member with the right tool to address it was just good sense.

He read on.

Process: Select your class. Your starting attributes will be customized to fit the class. Typical attributes for mortal humanoids range from 1 to 20. If you accept this offer, you will receive 100 points to assign as you see fit. After that, you’ll gain access to a selection of appropriate skills and gear.

The two descended a spiraling ramp into the merchant district, where the air smelled of roasted nuts, fresh bread, and alchemical solvents. A gnome workshop had set up shop in an alcove, the proprietor demonstrating a small mechanical bird that sang in three-part harmony. Nearby, a crowd of goblin children watched with rapt attention, their green faces illuminated by sparks flying from the gnome’s welding torch.

“Allow me tae recommend a few classes,” Alex said, brow furrowing as he navigated them around a troll hauling an enormous crate of dried fish.

Starting Class Options

• Shamanic Biologist: Work with spirits and elemental powers to augment and manipulate life. This path would allow you to commune with nature and see the invisible world. As you advance, it would grant you the ability to bestow unique shamanic benefits to your offspring.

• Lifeweb Guardian: A Lifeweb Guardian is a hybrid combat/healer type. Their magic allows them to connect to allies and manipulate health. Frequent use will unlock additional applications, including offensive options that include the transmission of diseases and curses. Given your unique genetic inheritance, this class may eventually synergize with your Heritage Chain bloodline gift.

• Sleepless Juggernaut Want to keep things simple? How about a class that lets you bypass sleep and do all sorts of... uh, activities, for countless hours? You’d get a ton more hit points, resilience, and some interesting combat options that would eventually allow you to sap the stamina and strength of others.

Mike considered the options, discarding Sleepless Juggernaut immediately. There was simple, then there was brainless. On the opposite side, Shamanic Biologist sounded like the type of path that would take him away from his core focus of helping people. One thing Thomas had taught all of them was that even as the biological savior of dying species, making the focus about sex was a mistake.

The heart was always compassion, love, and family.

Toward that end, Lifeweb Guardian made the most sense. “I’ll take that one,” he said, pointing. At his selection, the TRT updated.

Unique Attributes: Your attributes are a combination of your genetic heritage and class. Strength, Grace, and Intellect compare with standard humanoid norms. You’ve inherited Troll’s Toughness, which will make you far more resistant to damage and grant you modest regenerative properties.
